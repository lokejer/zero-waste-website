Nada is a package-free grocery store based in Vancouver, Canada. Though it is no longer open, it is a prime example of a successful zero waste community.
Their digital presence and transparent mission attract eco-conscious consumers. They also emphasise community engagement, educational events, and data tracking to reduce waste.
Co-founder Alison Carr brought her passion for food systems and community engagement to the venture, helping to operationalize Nada's mission of connecting people to a just and regenerative food system.
Kamikatsu, a small mountain town in Japan, gained global recognition for its pioneering zero-waste policy. Since 2003, residents have committed to sorting waste into over 40 categories for maximum recycling and reuse.
The town’s success lies in strong community involvement and education. Workshops, volunteer programmes, and consistent communication have empowered residents to adopt sustainable habits and take pride in their collective environmental impact.
Kamikatsu’s zero-waste centre, built from recycled materials, symbolises its vision. It includes a thrift store, reuse shop, and learning spaces, turning waste into opportunity and inspiring eco-conscious visitors from around the world.
Despite initial resistance, Kamikatsu now boasts recycling rates above 80%, far exceeding Japan’s national average. Its model shows how small, committed communities can lead global conversations on sustainability and responsible consumption.
